## Durable Lessons

- Separate "should we make it?" from "can we make it?" Rami Ismail's prototype/vertical-slice distinction maps cleanly to Riot's incubation/prototype split: prototypes validate the thesis and player response; vertical slices validate production pipeline, repeatability, and cost.
- Gate on explicit thesis, audience, and proof targets before building scale. Riot says only about 10% of pitches move into prototype, and no pitch passes on first attempt; the point is not bureaucracy, but forcing debate, research, comparison, and a narrow thesis before a team grows.
- The best kill gates combine autonomy with pre-agreed standards. Supercell lets teams decide before data exists, but once soft-launch metrics exist the "players" take over through pre-set goals. This avoids executive taste overriding evidence, and avoids post-hoc metric negotiation.
- Concept death needs a human transition ritual, not just a Jira status. Supercell's public postmortems and toast, and Riot's project reboot plan, both treat cancellation as a people event with timing, explanation, placement, and emotional aftermath.
- A kill is healthier when the next home for people is planned before the announcement. Riot's Zebra reboot had transition planning, one-on-ones, recruiting/HR support, and rapid placement targets; this is more operationally useful than a vague "learn from failure" slogan.
- Small teams work when responsibility is paired with quality bars and transparency. Supercell's "small cells" are not permission to drift; they come with brutal release standards, company-wide metric visibility, and willingness to kill work that does not meet the bar.
- Early Access can be a production instrument only if the game is designed for it. Supergiant designed Hades around modular structure, serial narrative, community feedback, regular updates, and enough quality at launch to make feedback useful rather than merely pointing out missing basics.
- Healthy production sometimes requires more time, more money, or less scope. Double Fine's Psychonauts 2 reporting shows the same project cutting boss fights under budget pressure, restoring them after Microsoft funding, and publicly claiming no crunch. The lesson is not "funding fixes production"; it is that sustainable schedules require real scope/funding tradeoffs.
- AAA polish has real opportunity cost. Naughty Dog reporting shows how a culture of perfectionism can create exceptional detail while also producing rework, attrition, implicit crunch, and fragile onboarding. The Last of Us Online cancellation shows another AAA tradeoff: a live-service ambition can consume the studio identity it was supposed to extend.
- Flat structure is not the same as accountable decision-making. Valve's handbook is useful for customer focus, temporary cabals, peer review, and self-directed ownership, but critical reporting points to hidden hierarchy, stack-ranking bias, and diversity risk when power is informal.
- Function-first prototypes beat character/theme-first invention. Splatoon's public prototype history is useful because the core territory-inking interaction came before the final squid identity. The character solved a mechanics/fiction problem rather than acting as the origin of the project.
- Crunch avoidance is not only ethical; it is an outcome risk control. The Game Outcomes Project found crunch correlated negatively with outcomes, including schedule performance, while design risk management and team focus correlated strongly with better results.

## Risks/Misreadings

- Do not copy Supercell's kill rate without Supercell's runway, recruiting bar, data scale, and mobile soft-launch context. "Kill fast" can become waste if the team lacks capital, replacement opportunities, and learning extraction.
- Do not copy Riot's gates as heavy governance. Riot's useful pattern is thesis-driven narrowing and people-centric cancellation; in a small repo this should become lightweight written proof, not a committee simulator.
- Do not copy Valve's flatness as "no roles." The useful pattern is temporary project structure around shipped value. The anti-pattern is unowned decisions, hidden power, and peer incentives that punish invisible work.
- Do not copy Naughty Dog's detail bar without also copying the cost accounting. "Make it 10% better" is not free; without a stop rule it becomes implicit overtime, late rework, and senior attrition.
- Do not treat Early Access as public QA for an unfinished promise. Supergiant entered Early Access with a game built for community iteration; a repo framework should require a feedback-ready build and a response cadence before external exposure.
- Do not romanticize documentary transparency. Double Fine's PsychOdyssey is unusually valuable, but filming struggle is not the same as resolving it. Use the visible decision points, not the drama, as operating material.
- Do not let metrics replace taste or love for the game. Supercell's Hay Day Pop lesson is that metric-following without genre conviction can still produce a game the team should kill.
- Do not overfit to winners. Most sources are from studios with hits, platforms, or publisher backing. Treat every lesson as a constraint to test, not a universal law.

## Repo Implications

- The repo's proof gates should make the prototype/vertical-slice distinction sharper: prototype gates ask "is the player promise real?"; vertical-slice gates ask "can the team produce this quality repeatedly at known cost?"
- Direction artifacts should include a Riot-style thesis with explicit "not doing" clauses. This would reduce feature debates caused by different hidden assumptions about audience, opportunity, and competitive position.
- Production should add a cancellation/reboot protocol. A killed concept should produce a short decision note, lessons, salvage list, emotional/ownership closeout, and next-placement plan for active contributors or agents.
- Evidence gates should distinguish pre-data judgment from post-data judgment. Before external playtest or telemetry, the accountable cell/owner decides. After agreed player evidence exists, the gate should bind itself to the evidence unless a waiver is recorded.
- Team-health checks belong in production gates, not retrospectives only. If a proof target requires extended overtime, vague ownership, or repeated negative feedback loops, the gate should treat that as production evidence against continuing unchanged.
- The framework should prefer temporary "cabals" with named leads and file/artifact ownership over permanent hierarchy. Temporary structure is useful; invisible authority is not.
- The repo's philosophy should keep "small team" language tied to responsibility, transparency, and stop conditions. Smallness alone does not create quality.
- External/public proof should require a Supergiant-style feedback contract: what feedback is desired, how often updates will be considered, what is out of scope, and when the team will pause to recharge.

## Concrete Changes To Propose

- Add a "Prototype Question" template field: `Should this game exist? What player/audience/thesis assumption would prove or disprove it?`
- Add a "Vertical Slice Production Test" field: `What is the second repeatable unit, how long did it take, and what pipeline changed after the first unit?`
- Add a "Kill/Pivot/Reboot Record" template with sections for decision trigger, evidence, owner verdict, salvageable work, people/agent reassignment, and next review date.
- Add a "Pre-Agreed Gate Metrics" rule for any soft-launch-like or external playtest gate: define success/failure thresholds before exposing the build.
- Add a "Tepid Response Protocol": if playtesters understand the game but do not want another run, the default next action is cut/pivot diagnosis, not feature expansion.
- Add a "Pride Bar" question borrowed from Supercell's failure lessons: `Even if this works, would we be proud to ship it as representative of the project?`
- Add a "Crunch Waiver" or "Sustainability Blocker" to milestone contracts: any plan requiring overtime must be recorded as a production risk, not hidden as commitment.
- Add a "Temporary Cabal Contract" for substantial work: purpose, lead-as-coordinator, decision owner, contributors, artifact boundaries, and dissolve condition.
- Add a "What Not To Copy" note to studio references so future docs do not cite Supercell, Valve, Naughty Dog, or Nintendo as simplistic role models.
- Add a "Feedback-Ready Public Proof" checklist before demos/Early Access-style exposure: playable quality, known gaps, feedback channels, update cadence, response owner, and pause/recharge plan.

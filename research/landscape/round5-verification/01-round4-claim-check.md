## Supported

- GDC 2026 supports these exact claims: the report is based on more than 2,300 game industry professionals; 36% of game industry professionals said they use generative AI tools as part of their job; 30% of respondents at game studios reported using AI tools; 58% of publishing/support/marketing/PR respondents reported using AI tools; common uses included research/brainstorming at 81%, daily tasks and code assistance at 47% each, and prototyping at 35%; 52% said generative AI has a negative impact on the industry, and 7% said positive. Use the GDC page rather than a PDF mirror when possible. Source: https://gdconf.com/article/gdc-2026-state-of-the-game-industry-reveals-impact-of-layoffs-generative-ai-and-more/
- Roblox supports the 44% claim, but only with its footnote intact: "top 1,000 creators" means creators ranked by Robux spent in experience over the past 28 days as of 2026-04-07, and the measured usage window was 2026-03-06 to 2026-04-07. It supports "Roblox says 44% of top creators used Assistant or MCP features in that window," not "44% of Roblox creators use AI." Source: https://about.roblox.com/newsroom/2026/04/roblox-studio-going-agentic
- Steam supports the policy claim that developers must complete a Content Survey before review and, if their game used AI services during development or at runtime, describe the implementation. Valve distinguishes pre-generated AI content from live-generated AI content and requires guardrail descriptions for live-generated AI. Source: https://partner.steamgames.com/doc/gettingstarted/contentsurvey
- The Steam AI count is supported only as third-party analysis, not Valve-published platform statistics: Tom's Hardware reports Ichiro Lambe / Totally Human Media found 7,818 Steam titles disclosing GenAI use, about 7% of the library, with about 20% of 2025 releases disclosing GenAI use as of July 2025. VGC reports the same basic finding as "nearly 8,000" and "20% of all games released so far in 2025." Sources: https://www.tomshardware.com/video-games/pc-gaming/1-in-5-steam-games-released-in-2025-use-generative-ai-up-nearly-700-percent-year-on-year-7-818-titles-disclose-genai-asset-usage-7-percent-of-entire-steam-library and https://www.videogameschronicle.com/news/steam-games-disclosing-generative-ai-use-are-up-800-this-year/
- Game Outcomes Project supports the direction of the Round 4 lesson: design risk management had the strongest category correlation reported, team focus came second, and avoidance of crunch came third. The article reports design risk management over 0.57, team focus at 0.50, and avoidance of crunch at 0.44, with crunch showing negative correlations including with project delays. Source: https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/the-game-outcomes-project-part-3-game-development-factors
- Supercell support is strong for small empowered teams, high quality bars, team-owned pre-data kill decisions, pre-agreed soft-launch goals, and treating failure as a learning event. The GamesBeat interview directly supports the team-before-data / players-after-soft-launch split and pre-set retention goals. Game Developer supports the "last 10 games: seven prototype kills, two soft-launch kills, one global launch" example. Sources: https://gamesbeat.com/supercell-ceo-thrives-on-trusting-the-instincts-of-game-developers/ and https://www.gamedeveloper.com/business/quality-is-worth-killing-for-supercell-s-ruthless-approach-to-production
- Riot support is strong for thesis-driven incubation and prototype gates. Riot says approximately 10% of pitches make it into Prototype and no pitch had passed on first attempt in that 2020 post. Riot also supports prototype deliverables around audience definition, representative playable gameplay, risky innovation proof, team learning, and playtest evidence. Sources: https://www.riotgames.com/en/r-and-d-office/incubation-exploration-with-a-plan and https://www.riotgames.com/en/r-and-d-office/prototype-building-a-games-substance
- Naughty Dog support is adequate for source-sensitive cautionary language: Kotaku's 2020 report documents crunch, perfectionism, attrition, implicit overtime pressure, and production rework through interviews with current/former developers; Naughty Dog's own 2023 update supports the live-service tradeoff claim that The Last of Us Online would have required years of post-launch support and a studio identity/resource shift. GDC 2026 says 36% of game industry professionals use generative AI at work; Google Cloud says 90% of 615 surveyed game developers use AI in workflows; Steam disclosure analysis reports rapid growth in disclosed GenAI content. These are different populations, definitions, and methods. Sources: https://gdconf.com/article/gdc-2026-state-of-the-game-industry-reveals-impact-of-layoffs-generative-ai-and-more/ and https://cloud.google.com/resources/games-report
- Google Cloud's 90% claim is supported as Google Cloud / Harris Poll research based on 615 developers, but should be cited as vendor-sponsored survey data and not generalized to all developers or compared one-to-one with GDC's generative-AI job-use result. Source: https://cloud.google.com/resources/games-report
- Steam Next Fest backlash is supported as press-reported backlash and user criticism, not as a measured player sentiment finding. TechSpot cites complaints about AI-created assets and Steam Next Fest discovery, but this should not become "players broadly reject AI games." Source: https://www.techspot.com/news/106974-steam-faces-backlash-promoting-excessive-ai-created-games.html
- Supercell's Hay Day Pop lesson supports "metric-following without genre conviction can be a failure mode," but the phrasing should be attributed to Supercell's own retrospective and not treated as a universal law. Source: https://supercell.com/en/news/learning-from-failures/

It does not prove that all cancellations should be handled this way, because it depends on Riot-scale internal mobility. Source: https://www.riotgames.com/en/r-and-d-office/game-over-good-game

## Overstated Or Needs Hedge

- Avoid "GDC says 52% of developers' companies use generative AI" unless the full report page is cited directly for that exact metric. The official GDC article checked here supports 36% job use, 30% studio-respondent use, and 52% negative impact sentiment.
- Avoid "one in five Steam games use generative AI" without "released in 2025," "disclosed," and "according to a July 2025 third-party analysis." The defensible wording is about disclosed GenAI use, not actual use.
- Avoid "Steam disclosures are voluntary" as a standalone claim. Valve requires the Content Survey and requires detailed AI disclosure if AI was used. The weak point is not formal optionality; it is reliance on developer self-reporting and third-party scraping/analysis.
- Avoid "AI-created Steam games caused backlash" as a broad market conclusion. Use "press reported user backlash around AI-heavy Steam Next Fest discovery surfaces."
- Avoid "Game Outcomes Project proves crunch makes games worse." It reports correlations from a 2014 survey and explicitly frames project outcomes through correlation analysis. Use "correlated with worse outcomes" and avoid causal language.
- Avoid treating Supercell's kill rate as a model to copy. The evidence is real, but the context is mobile, high-runway, high-talent-density, soft-launch, and metric-rich.
- Avoid "Riot's gates prove committees are best." Riot's sources support thesis-driven narrowing and playable proof; they do not support heavy governance as a universal small-team pattern.
- Avoid "Naughty Dog proves AAA polish causes crunch." The safer claim is that reporting on Naughty Dog illustrates how perfectionism, late rework, implicit overtime, and weak stop rules can coexist with exceptional craft and create production risk.
- Avoid "accessibility tags make games accessible." Tags communicate features pre-purchase; they do not validate implementation quality unless backed by testing and honest store metadata.
- Avoid "AI slop is always worthless." "Why Slop Matters" explicitly argues slop should be studied seriously and may have social/aesthetic functions. The repo-safe lesson is about review burden, superficial competence, and mass producibility, not blanket dismissal.

## Dropped Claims

- Drop any uncited or mirror-only GDC 2026 numbers not present in the official GDC article unless the official full report is archived in-repo or directly cited.
- Drop any claim that Steam's AI disclosure counts are official Valve totals. They are third-party counts based on public disclosures.
- Drop any claim that 44% of all Roblox creators use AI. The supported claim is limited to top creators by Robux spent in experience over a specific 2026 measurement window.
- Drop any causal claim that crunch, team focus, or design risk management independently causes commercial success. Game Outcomes Project supports correlations, not causal proof.
- Drop any universal claim that Supercell-style game killing is appropriate for all teams or genres.
- Drop any claim that accessibility/localization platform metadata is enough to satisfy player needs. It must be matched to shipped build behavior, QA/LQA, support, and honest disclosure.
- Drop any broad claim that AI slop has already flooded all game discovery surfaces. The supported evidence is narrower: Steam disclosure counts rose sharply in a third-party analysis, and press reported AI-heavy discovery complaints in Steam Next Fest.

## Repo-Safe Wording

- "GDC 2026 reported that 36% of surveyed game industry professionals use generative AI tools as part of their job, while 52% said generative AI is having a negative impact on the industry. The survey covered more than 2,300 people across the broader game ecosystem, so use role- and sample-aware wording."
- "Google Cloud's 2025 vendor-sponsored survey, conducted by The Harris Poll with 615 developers, reported 90% AI workflow usage. Treat this as one adoption signal, not the industry baseline."
- "A July 2025 third-party analysis reported that 7,818 Steam titles disclosed GenAI use and that about 20% of 2025 releases had disclosed GenAI use at that point. Steam's own policy confirms AI disclosure fields exist, but the count is not an official Valve metric."
- "Roblox stated that 44% of its top 1,000 creators, ranked by Robux spent in experience, used Assistant or MCP features between 2026-03-06 and 2026-04-07."
- "The Game Outcomes Project found strong correlations between better project outcomes and design risk management, team focus, and avoidance of crunch in its 2014 survey. Use the result as evidence for production risk review, not as causal proof."
- "Supercell is a useful reference for small empowered teams, pre-agreed soft-launch goals, and high quality bars, but its kill-rate lessons should be adapted cautiously outside mobile soft-launch contexts."
- "Riot R&D is a useful reference for thesis/audience clarity, prototype proof, and people-aware reboot handling. In this repo, translate that into lightweight written proof, not big-studio governance."
- "Naughty Dog is a cautionary case for the cost side of AAA polish: reporting and first-party updates support the risks of perfectionism, live-service scope, rework, attrition, and implicit crunch."
- "Accessibility and localization claims should be treated as release promises. Steam language metadata affects store presentation and search; Xbox/Microsoft accessibility guidance and tags help communicate and test features, but shipped behavior and player-facing disclosure must match."
- "AI slop should be framed as a review-economy and authorship risk: outputs can look competent, be cheap to mass-produce, and shift sorting costs onto reviewers, maintainers, players, and storefronts."

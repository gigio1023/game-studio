# Direction Gates

Direction gates protect the game identity.

## GS-ENTRY-ROUTE

Question: Does the project have an explicit entry route before Direction Lock?

Review:

- Entry mode is one of `direction-carry-in`, `taste-to-game`, or
  `brownfield-unknown`.
- Direction source is recorded or explicitly unknown.
- Creator fluency and authority owner are recorded.
- Current concept status is recorded.
- First required artifact and first proof target are named.
- Allowed agent autonomy is bounded.
- Next skill or gate and stop condition are explicit.

## GS-TASTE-CLARITY

Question: Is the creator's taste clear enough to shape concepts without claiming
ownership of it?

Review:

- Reference moments name what mattered, not only titles.
- Taste and anti-taste are separated.
- Desired emotion and undesired friction are separated.
- Hard noes are explicit.
- Agent inferences are marked as inferences.
- Contradictions are preserved instead of smoothed away too early.
- The next step does not require the creator to already know genre, core loop,
  engine, monetization, or release strategy.

## GS-CONCEPT-SLATE

Question: Are the concepts comparable, rejectable, and proofable?

Review:

- The slate contains 3 to 5 concepts unless the authority owner requested a
  narrower set.
- Each concept names target player, player fantasy, core verb, intended emotion,
  distinctive bet, main risk, first playable proof, and reason to reject.
- Concepts are compared by taste fit, proofability, production risk, content
  burden, and implementation path.
- Parked and rejected concepts have reasons.
- The slate does not become active scope without a user decision.
- The output recommends at most one next concept and one next proof.

## GS-CREATIVE-BRIEF

Question: Is one selected or candidate concept directional enough to plan a
first playable proof?

Review:

- The brief names player promise, target player, player fantasy, core verb, core
  pressure, intended emotion, distinctive bet, and main risk.
- Creative pillars and anti-pillars can reject work.
- First playable proof and proof budget are explicit.
- Work to ignore for now is listed.
- If the user has not accepted the concept, the brief is marked as candidate
  material.
- Direction Lock is deferred until a decision record exists.

## GS-CONSTRAINT-LADDER

Question: Has the concept been bounded into a finishable proof path?

Review:

- Capacity, taste, experience, mechanic, content, and release constraints are
  named.
- The proof budget states concrete limits for time, team capacity, tools, core
  verbs, systems, content count, and capture or review cost.
- Must-survive elements preserve the user's taste and selected player fantasy.
- Cut-first candidates and parked ambition have revisit conditions.
- The first proof path names the prototype question, smallest playable loop,
  required capture, review gate, and stop condition.
- Tradeoffs that change the selected concept, public promise, or proof budget
  require a decision record.

## GS-PROTOTYPE-QUESTION

Question: Does the first prototype test one named risk?

Review:

- Prototype question is specific enough to pass, fail, or change direction.
- Player moment to prove is named.
- What to build and what to ignore are bounded.
- What to capture is explicit.
- Minimum playable loop is small enough to finish.
- Review roles are named.
- Stop condition and direction-change trigger are explicit.
- If the question requires taste judgment or concept selection, the terminal
  outcome is `USER_DECISION_NEEDED`.

## GS-THESIS

Question: Does the game thesis define a specific game identity?

Review:

- The player role is concrete.
- The core pressure is playable, not only thematic.
- The core decision and feedback loop are visible.
- The target audience is stated or clear from context.
- The thesis rejects at least one tempting wrong direction.

## GS-PILLARS

Question: Are the creative pillars falsifiable and useful?

Review:

- Each pillar can reject a feature.
- Pillars do not overlap.
- Pillars are visible in gameplay, UI, content, or production choices.
- Anti-pillars exist.

## GS-PLAYER-EXPERIENCE

Question: Does the plan specify what the player feels and understands over time?

Review:

- 5-minute, 10-minute, and 20-minute targets exist.
- Mechanics, dynamics, and intended experience are connected.
- Confusion, mystery, friction, and failure are bounded.
- The first thing the player must learn is explicit.
- Meaningful choice and feedback readability can be playtested.
- The target experience can be tested in a playtest.

## GS-AI-AUTHORITY

Question: If the game uses AI, who owns truth and consequences?

Review:

- AI generation boundaries are explicit.
- Deterministic systems own state transitions, scoring, law, economy, combat, or end states.
- Failures and fallbacks are designed.
- Generated outputs have provenance, rejection, and review rules.
- Player-facing AI requirements are honest.
